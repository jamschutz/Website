Movie Tie-In Games and Adaptations
Adaptations have existed for thousands of years: from Shakespeare, to Joyce, and, yes, to 1983’s E.T.: The Extraterrestrial for the Atari 2600. Adaptations hold a tenuous place in culture, almost universally disdained and devalued. And yet, they are seemingly everywhere: the MCU is unavoidable, every major video game IP is getting a TV series, Mario has a theme park, and Netflix has a show based on r/twosentencehorror. Like it or not, adaptations have achieved staying power, and it’s only fair to assume this has happened because they hold some fascination with audiences, what Linda Hutcheon calls “repetition with variation.” Movie tie-in games, in particular, have achieved a particularly pointed form of infamy, relegated to the dustbin of willfully forgotten history. But there exist bright spots in these works – GoldenEye, Spider-Man 2, and King Kong, to name a few – and lessons for the strengths and follies of games as a medium.
The goal for this class is to examine adaptation within a video game format. We will discuss the nature of adaptation, and the aesthetic effects of intertextuality. Each week we’ll watch a film, and in class we’ll play its video game adaptation, breaking down the particular challenges, triumphs, or failures of these works, and investigating what they reveal to us about video games at large. What does moving these stories from one form to another reveal? Finally, we’ll seek to understand the many modes of adaptation itself: traditional narrative adaptations like Peter Jackson’s King Kong, adaptations of worlds like Alien: Isolation, pastiche in works like Uncharted 2 and Until Dawn, and more “fuzzy” adaptations like in Oikospiel. By the end of the class, students will have a grasp on the broad histories of licensed video games, the many modes of adaptations and their effects, and have created their own movie adaptations in an engine of their choice (no code required).
This class is for you if you:
  • are interested in the intersections between games, play, and cinema
  • want to make & share your own adaptations alongside others
  • want to meet in a physical space to discuss art & games with others
  • want an excuse to play through 1983’s E.T.: The Extraterrestrial
We will be making a video game adaptation of a movie in this class, but there is no expectation of having a programming background – we'll cover no-code game engines as well (though if you do have a programming background, you are free to use whatever engines you like)
